Deterministic Chaos: Discord-Style Tags for Ephemeral Users

Shoan Bhatambare · 2 min read · Handwritten

ephemeral · lasting or used for only a short period of time

Leaving behind a mark is the less focused part of being ephemeral, whether we talk about our mortality or the systems we create.

How do we differentiate users if they can occupy the same names?

Allowing reusable identities brings unique challenges. Which Albert was responsible for e=mc² and which was discussing politics with you on Conversatio? Wouldn't it be obvious if we could look at their faces, surnames, or something even simpler, their Tags.

The #Tag Problem

4 digits give you 10,000 different possibilities for each unique username, which is a perfectly fine design choice for initial launch. Hell, it was good enough for discord's discriminator system till 2023[1]. And to go from #0000 to #9999 linearly, in technical implementation, is as easy as eating an apple.

But when you remove the forbiddenness of the fruit[2], there's added room for creativity.

While linear incrementation follows natural rule, it falls short in aesthetics. Sequenced tags would have given away the number of signups while also being boring.

So if linearity is a no go, the next obvious choice would be to go random. Roll a dice and check if the number is already used or not, sounds simple enough but the compounding nature of database lookups and increasing storage space for used tags quickly thwarts this approach as well. We had to make the numbers seem random.

True Random or Just Math?

Imagine a clock with ten thousand ticks. You could jump 3 ticks and generate unique tags which only repeat after exhausting all ticks on the clock. But 0000, 0003, 0006, 0009... is not an exciting sequence. The reason why jumping 3 instead of 2 or 4 ticks works is because 3 is co prime[3] to 10000, meaning they share no common factors other than 1. Even though it's simple, lets write down this algorithm[4] to make it easier to tweak:

tag = (counter * x) % 10000; where x is the co prime.

The % 10000 operation covers cases where the result of the previous multiplication goes over 10000.

Algoesthetics

So now its just a matter of choosing the x. Each co prime builds its own pattern around the clock; 9999 would count backwards while 5001 would alternate between two halves of the space. We wanted a stride which would feel random, with no obvious pattern to the human eye.

Behold, 7723. It takes the crown when it comes to being messy with the distribution. The numbers it generated looked beautifully unpredictable. The math feels like magic, a quiet sleight of hand against our ability for pattern recognition.

0000, 7723, 5446, 3169, 0892...

Technical Analysis

The equation is essentially a minimal perfect hash function[5]. All we need to store in memory is a counter for each name, bumping it up after each use basically taking O(1) in space complexity. The math guarantees no collisions and zero retries making every case a best case scenario.

It's a perfect system operating on an understood limit of 10,000 signups for a particular name. Even the very nature of ephemerality, the fact that something which is gone still might have an effect on what might come, should drive innovation in whatever path we take.

"Own Your Tag."
